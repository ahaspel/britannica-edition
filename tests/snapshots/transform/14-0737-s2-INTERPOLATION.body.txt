PAGE:737(from Lat. «I»interpolare«/I», to alter, or insert
something fresh, connected with «I»polire«/I», a polish), in mathematics,
the process of obtaining intermediate terms of a series
of which particular terms only are given. The cubes, for instance,
shown in the second column of the accompanying table, may
be regarded as terms of a series, and the cube of a fractional
number, not exceeding the last number in the first column,
may be found by interpolation. The process of obtaining the
cube of a number exceeding the last number in the first column
would be «I»extrapolation«/I»; the formulae which apply to interpolation
apply in theory to extrapolation, but in practice
special precautions as to accuracy are necessary. The present
article deals only with interpolation.

The term is usually limited to those cases in which there
are two quantities, «I»x«/I» and «I»u«/I», which are so related that when «I»x«/I»
has any arbitrary value, lying perhaps between certain limits,
the value of «I»u«/I» is determinate. There is a given series of associated
values of «I»u«/I» and of «I»x«/I», and interpolation consists in determining
the value of «I»u«/I» for any arbitrary value of «I»x«/I», or the value of «I»x«/I» for
any arbitrary value of «I»u«/I», lying between two of the values in the
series. Either of the two quantities may be regarded as a function
of the other; it is convenient to treat one, «I»x«/I», as the “independent
variable,” the other, «I»u«/I», being treated as the “dependent variable,”
«I»i.e.«/I» as a function of «I»x«/I». If, as is usually the case, the successive
values of one of the quantities proceed by a constant increment,
this quantity is to be regarded as the independent variable.
The two series of values may be tabulated, those of «I»x«/I» being
placed in a column (or row), and those of «I»u«/I» in a parallel column
(or row); «I»u«/I» is then said to be «I»tabulated in terms of x«/I». The independent
variable «I»x«/I» is called the «I»argument«/I», and the dependent
variable «I»u«/I» is called the «I»entry«/I». Interpolation, in the ordinary
sense, consists in determining the value of «I»u«/I» for a value of «I»x«/I»
intermediate between two values appearing in the table. This
may be described as «I»direct interpolation«/I», to distinguish it from
«I»inverse interpolation«/I», which consists in determining the value of
«I»x«/I» for a value of «I»u«/I» intermediate between two in the table. The
methods employed can be extended to cases in which the value
of «I»u«/I» depends on the values of two or more independent quantities
«I»x«/I», «I»y«/I»...

In the ordinary case we may regard the values of «I»x«/I» as measured
along a straight line OX from a fixed point O, so that to any value
of «I»x«/I» there corresponds a point on the line. If we represent the
corresponding value of «I»u«/I» by an ordinate drawn from the line, the
extremities of all such ordinates will lie on a curve which will be the
graph of «I»u«/I» with regard to «I»x«/I». Interpolation therefore consists in
determining the length of the ordinate of a curve occupying a
particular position, when the lengths of ordinates occupying certain
specified positions are known. If «I»u«/I» is a function of two variables,
«I»x«/I» and «I»y«/I», we may similarly represent it by the ordinate of a surface,
the position of the ordinate being determined by the values of «I»x«/I»
and of «I»y«/I» jointly.

The series or tables to which interpolation has to be applied may
for convenience be regarded as falling into two main groups. The
first group comprises mathematical tables, «I»i.e.«/I» tables of mathematical
functions; in the case of such a table the value of the function u
for each tabulated value of «I»x«/I» is calculated to a known degree of
accuracy, and the degree of accuracy of an interpolated value of u
can be estimated. The second group comprises tables of values
which are found experimentally, «I»e.g.«/I» values of a physical quantity
or of a statistical ratio; these values are usually subject to certain
“errors” of observation or of random selection (see «LN:Probability|Probability«/LN»).
The methods of interpolation are usually the same in the two groups
of cases, but special considerations have to be taken into account
in the second group. The line of demarcation of the two groups is
not absolutely fixed; the tables used by actuaries, for instance,
which are of great importance in practical life, are based on statistical
observations, but the tables formed directly from the observations
have been “smoothed” so as to obtain series which correspond in
form to the series of values of mathematical functions.

It must be assumed, at any rate in the case of a mathematical
function, that the “entry” «I»u«/I» varies continuously with the “argument”
«I»x«/I», «I»i.e.«/I» that there are no sudden breaks, changes of direction,
&c., in the curve which is the graph of «I»u«/I».

Various methods of interpolation are described below. The
simplest is that which uses the «I»principle of proportional parts«/I»;
and mathematical tables are usually arranged so as to enable this
method to be employed. Where this is not possible, the methods are
based either on the use of Taylor’s Theorem, which gives a formula
involving differential coefficients (see «LN:Infinitesimal Calculus|Infinitesimal Calculus«/LN»),
or on the properties of finite differences (see «LN:Differences, Calculus of|Differences, Calculus of«/LN»).
Taylor’s Theorem can only be applied directly to a known
mathematical function; but it can be applied indirectly, by means
of finite differences, in various cases where the form of the function
expressing «I»u«/I» in terms of «I»x«/I» is unknown; and even where the form
of this function is known it is sometimes more convenient to determine
the differential coefficients by means of the differences than
to calculate them directly from their mathematical expressions.
Finally, there are cases where we cannot even employ finite-difference
formulae directly. In these cases we must adopt some special
method; «I»e.g.«/I» we may instead of «I»u«/I» tabulate some function of «I»u«/I»,
such as its logarithm, which is found to be amenable to ordinary
processes, then determine the value of this function corresponding
to the particular value of «I»x«/I», and thence determine the corresponding
value of «I»u«/I» itself.

In considering methods of interpolation, it will be assumed,
unless the contrary is stated, that the values of «I»x«/I» proceed by a
constant increment, which will be denoted by «I»h«/I».

In order to see what method is to be employed, it is usually
necessary to arrange the given series of values of «I»u«/I» in the form of a
table, as explained above, and then to take the successive «I»differences«/I»
of «I»u«/I». The differences of the successive values of «I»u«/I» are called its
«I»first differences«/I»; these form a new series, the first differences of
which are the «I»second differences«/I» of «I»u«/I»; and so on. The systems of
notation of the differences are explained briefly below. For the
fuller discussion, reference should be made to «LN:Differences, Calculus of|Differences, Calculus of«/LN».

In Example 1 the first difference of «I»u«/I» corresponding to a difference
of «I»h«/I» ≡.001 in «I»x«/I» is.0001000; but, since we are working throughout
to seven places of decimals, it is more convenient to write it 1000.
This system of ignoring the decimal point in dealing with differences
will be adopted throughout this article. To find «I»u«/I» for an intermediate
value of «I»x«/I» we assume the principle of proportional parts,
«I»i.e.«/I» we assume that the difference in «I»u«/I» is proportional to the difference
in «I»x«/I». Thus for «I»x«/I» = 4.342945 the difference in «I»u«/I» is.945 of 1000 = 945,
so that «I»u«/I» is.6376898 +.0000945 =.6377843. For «I»x«/I» = 4.34294482
the difference in «I»u«/I» would be 944.82, so that the value of «I»u«/I» would
apparently be.6376898 +.000094482 =.637784282. This, however,
would be incorrect. It must be remembered that the values of
«I»u«/I» are only given “correct to seven places of decimals,” «I»i.e.«/I» eachPAGE:738tabulated value differs from the corresponding true value by a «I»tabular«/I»
«I»error«/I» which may have any value up to ± ½ of.0000001; and we
cannot therefore by interpolation obtain a result which is correct
to nine places. If the interpolated value of «I»u«/I» has to be used in
calculations for which it is important that this value should be as
accurate as possible, it may be convenient to retain it temporarily
in the form.6376898 + 944 82 =.6377842 82 or.6376898 + 944⁸² =
.6377842⁸²; but we must ultimately return to the seven-place
arrangement and write it as.6377843. The result of interpolation
by first difference is thus usually subject to two inaccuracies, the
first being the tabular error of «I»u«/I» itself, and the second being due to
the necessity of adjusting the final figure of the added (proportional)
difference. If the tabulated values are correct to seven places of
decimals, the interpolated value, with the final figure adjusted, will
be within.0000001 of its true value.

In Example 2 the differences do not at first sight appear to run
regularly, but this is only due to the fact that the final figure in
each value of «I»u«/I» represents, as explained in the last paragraph, an

we proceed is the same; but we use the actual difference corresponding
to the interval in which the value of «I»x«/I» lies. Thus for «I»x«/I» = 7.41373
we should have «I»u«/I» =.86982 + (.373 of 58) =.87004; this result

«CTR»«I»Interpolation by Substituted Tabulation.«/I»«/CTR»

10. The relation of «I»u«/I» to «I»x«/I» may be such that the successive differences
of «I»u«/I» increase rapidly, so that interpolation-formulae cannot
be employed directly. Other methods have then to be used. The
best method is to replace «I»u«/I» by some expression v which is a function
of «I»u«/I» such that (i.) the value of v or of «I»u«/I» can be determined for any
given value of «I»u«/I» or of v, and (ii.) when v is tabulated in terms of «I»x«/I»
the differences decrease rapidly. We can then calculate v, and
thence «I»u«/I», for any intermediate value of «I»x«/I».

If, for instance, we require tan «I»x«/I» for a value of «I»x«/I» which is nearly
90°, it will be found that the table of tangents is not suitable for
interpolation. We can, however, convert it into a table of cotangents
to about the same number of significant figures; from this we can
easily calculate cot «I»x«/I», and thence tan «I»x«/I».

11. This method is specially suitable for statistical data, where
the successive values of «I»u«/I» represent the area of a figure of frequency
up to successive ordinates. We have first to determine, by inspection,
a curve which bears a general similarity to the unknown
curve of frequency, and whose area and abscissa are so related that
either can be readily calculated when the other is known. This
may be called the «I»auxiliary curve«/I». Denoting by ξ the abscissa
of this curve which corresponds to area «I»u«/I», we find the value of ξ
corresponding to each of the given values of «I»u«/I». Then, tabulating
ξ in terms of «I»x«/I», we have a table in which, if the auxiliary curve
has been well chosen, differences of ξ after the first or second are
negligible. We can therefore find ξ, and thence «I»u«/I», for any intermediate
value of «I»x«/I».

14. «I»Interpolation from Tables of Double Entry.«/I»—When «I»u«/I» is a
function of «I»x«/I» and «I»y«/I», and is tabulated in terms of «I»x«/I» and of «I»y«/I» jointly,
its calculation for a pair of values not given in the table may be
effected either directly or by first forming a table of values of u
in terms of «I»y«/I» for the particular value of «I»x«/I» and then determining u
from this table for the particular value of y. For direct interpolation,
consider that Δ represents differencing by changing «I»x«/I» into «I»x«/I» + 1,
and Δ′ differencing by changing «I»y«/I» into «I»y«/I» + 1. Then the formula is

«CTR»«I»u«/I»x, y = (1 + Δ)x (1 + Δ′)y «I»u«/I»₀,₀;«/CTR»

and the right-hand side can be developed in whatever form is most
convenient for the particular case.
