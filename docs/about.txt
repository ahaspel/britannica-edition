About this Edition

The 11th edition of the Encyclopedia Britannia is a peak product of the peak of Western civilization, completed in 1911, shortly before the world went mad. 

This is the definitive online edition of Encyclopedia Britannica's legendary 11th edition, Jorge Luis Borges's favorite reference book, etc. It is the 11th that T.S. Eliot's "Soul curled up on the window seat reading the Encyclopædia Britannica" is curled up with. 

Although by the 11th edition Britannica was primarily a British operation, it betrays its Scottish origins in several amusing ways, such as the immensely detailed entry on BAG-PIPES. It had substantial contributions from many Americans, not least the financial one from Horace Everett Cooper, Britannica's publisher at the time. Hugh Chisholm gives a thorough account of the Encyclopedia's history in his Editorial Preface and I will not repeat it here.

Its contributors include such luminaries as Matthew Arnold, Bertrand Russell, J.B. Bury, George Saintsbury, Edmund Gosse, Alfred North Whitehead, and many more. The celebrities, however, are with a few exceptions beside the point. Russell, who by virtue of his fame is always cited, merely co-authored a single article, GEOMETRY. And the article on Arnold is far better than the single article by him, a far too generous assessment of the repugnant 19th-century French literary lion SAINTE-BEUVE.

The real heroes of the 11th, its major contributors, are mostly unknown today. They were largely but by no means exclusively academics, in an age when the term stood in for genuine scholarship and erudition. This was the age of the polymath, and many display surprisingly varied expertise. Kathleen Schlesinger, who wrote 93 articles, almost all on music, also contributed ORCHIDS. BASE-BALL, as American a subject as one could wish, was written by a French professor, Ernest Babelon, also responsible for CARTHAGE, FOIL-FENCING, and KITE-FLYING. Austin Dobson, by profession a civil engineer who became a principal at the Board of Trade, by reputation a poet who specialized in exotic verse forms, is in the 11th the author of various articles on 18th-century literary figures such as FIELDING, RICHARDSON, and CHESTERFIELD, as well as a co-author of JOHN LOCKE, neither literary nor of the 18th century.

The indefatigable Thomas Ashby is the 11th's leading contributor, at a staggering 236 articles. Ashby was an archaeologist, Director of the British School of Archaeology at Rome and author of countless books. At least half of his articles -- including AQUINAS, LUCREZIA BORGIA, CALENDAR, CASHEW NUT, CASINO, JAMES MONROE, SEVEN YEARS' WAR, SOLON, and TERENCE -- bear a dubious connection to archaeology or none at all.

At the opposite end are the authors who contribute a single article on a subject that they know everything about. Joseph Burton co-wrote FIREBRICK with another brick expert, William Burton (doubtless a relation). As a partner in Pilkington’s Tile and Pottery Co., Clifton Junction, Manchester, Joseph, one can be sure, knew his firebrick. Or Lyman Abbott, an American divine who co-edited The Christian Union with HENRY WARD BEECHER and succeeded him as pastor Plymouth Church. One could scarcely ask for a better source. My favorite in this category is an "Investigator of Atmospheric Dust. Inventor of instruments for counting the dust particles in the atmosphere. Author of papers on Dust Fogs and Clouds; Hazing Effects of Atmospheric Dust; Cyclones and Anticyclones; &c., in publications of Royal Society," one John Aitken, who knew more about DUST than I imagined it possible to know.

For literary merit the 11th is unparalleled. To the virgin reader I commend a few of my shorter favorites: Gosse on BLANK VERSE, J.B. Bury and Richard Lydekker on EDWARD GIBBON, J.H. Freese and Eduard Meyer on XENOPHON. The magisterial, collaborative, book-length treatises on EGYPT, JAPAN, FRANCE, and ROME can be browsed endlessly with profit. Best of all is simply to look up whatever topic interests you and start reading.

The best arguments for the 11th are inadvertently supplied, as so often, by its detractors. 



This version of the 11th enhances the original in several ways. It can be searched by text across the entire corpus and within article. The long articles begin with section links to facilitate internal navigation.

The scholarly apparatus is extensive. There are 1,403 named authors in the 11th, and the Contributors index gives the credentials, biographical entries, and articles written for each. Each article includes the author's full name, linked to his entry; the print edition displayed only initials, which a curious user was obliged to look up. Volume and page of the original are displayed in the left margin for ease of citation. Shoulder-sections are displayed at the right. When an article is based on source that has not been proofread or contains potential OCR transcription errors, this is also marked. Articles can be searched by title, and there is also full-text search for articles as well as the entire corpus.
